# Anti-Patterns

Successful entrepreneurs explicitly warn against these behaviors, which often disguise themselves as "work" but actually prevent entrepreneurs from finding real success.

## Playing Business
Spending time or money on things like websites, logos, LLCs, and C-level titles before having any paying customers.
**Why it fails:** It wastes resources on unvalidated ideas and distracts from the only thing that matters initially: getting someone to pay you $1.


## The Engineering Disease (Building Before Validating)
Building software, reading books, or taking courses before actually validating the idea with paying customers.
**Why it fails:** It creates an illusion of safety and delays the actual starting process. You end up building things people don't want or you don't know how to sell.


## The 'Next New Product' Curse
Believing that launching a brand new product will be the magic bullet for business growth.
**Why it fails:** It distracts from the core product that already has product/market fit, which usually drives the vast majority of revenue and has much more untapped potential.


## The Mom Response
Asking friends or family if they "would" buy your product instead of asking for actual payment.
**Why it fails:** They say it's a great idea to be supportive, but when it's time to actually pay, they make excuses. Never ask for hypothetical validation.
> "never ask people if they would buy it it's not the same cuz your friends it'll be like the mom response"


## Worrying About Stolen Ideas
Worrying about NDAs or people stealing your idea.
**Why it fails:** It prevents you from sharing your product and getting feedback. All businesses are copies anyway; execution is what matters.
> "all business is a copy of a business"


## Accepting Multiple Goals
Accepting multiple goals or maintaining massive lists of unfocused ideas.
**Why it fails:** It dilutes your focus and makes decision-making difficult. Relentless focus on a single goal is required to achieve massive results.


## Asking for Favors
Approaching influencers or partners by asking for favors without offering value.
**Why it fails:** Strangers don't owe you anything and don't care about your need for attention; you must offer them value (WIIFT) first.

## Constantly Starting New Projects
Constantly starting new projects instead of sticking with one.
**Why it fails:** You miss out on the compounding benefits of longevity. If something is working, you should make it work better rather than abandoning it for a new novelty.


## Waiting to Market
Waiting until the product is perfect or launched to start marketing.
**Why it fails:** It's driven by the fear of rejection. By the time you launch, you have no audience to sell to.


## Relying on Software Features
Relying on software features as your primary business moat.
**Why it fails:** AI tools can now easily replicate entire software platforms or generate custom code for businesses, making the software itself a commodity. The real moat is distribution and trust.


## Offering Vague Help
Offering vague help by asking "Can I help you with anything?"
**Why it fails:** It is too broad and puts the burden of figuring out the task on the person you are trying to help. You need to offer a specific service to make it easy for them to say yes or no.
